How to Build a Full Presentation Using Claude — A 10-Step Guide
This guide walks you through 10 prompts you can use with Claude to go from a blank page to a polished, well-structured presentation. Each step focuses on a different aspect of the process. Just replace the placeholders (like [topic]) with your own details.

Step 1: Create a Presentation Blueprint
Start by asking Claude to act as a presentation consultant. Give it your topic and ask for a full blueprint that covers the objective, target audience, key message, emotional arc, and slide flow. The goal here is to get a clear structure before writing anything — every section should have a reason to exist.
Step 2: Write an Opening Hook
Next, ask Claude to write 3 options for an opening that grabs attention in the first 10 seconds. You can ask it to use a surprising statistic, a bold question, or a pattern interrupt. Skip the "good morning, today we'll talk about..." — go straight into something that makes people look up from their phones.
Step 3: Build a Slide-by-Slide Script
Now ask Claude to write a full script for each slide. For every slide, request a headline, up to 3 bullet points, the exact words you'd say out loud, and a transition sentence that leads naturally into the next slide. Specify how many slides you want.
Step 4: Turn Data into a Story
If your presentation includes data, paste it into Claude and ask it to build a narrative around the numbers. It can tell you which stats to highlight, what story they support, what type of visual works best for each, and what to say when each number appears on screen.
Step 5: Prepare for Objections
List the objections or pushback you expect from your audience. Ask Claude to build a slide for each one that addresses the concern before anyone raises it. This makes your audience feel like you've already thought through every angle.
Step 6: Write an Executive Summary Slide
Ask Claude to distill the entire presentation into a single slide — under 60 words. It should cover the problem, the solution, the proof, and the ask. This is useful for stakeholders who want the bottom line up front.
Step 7: Write a Closing Slide with a Call to Action
Ask Claude to write your final slide and closing script. The ending should make the audience feel the cost of doing nothing, the importance of acting now, and give them one clear, simple next step.
Step 8: Prepare for Q&A
Ask Claude to generate the 10 hardest questions your specific audience might ask, along with confident, concise answers. Also ask for a few "bridging phrases" — short sentences you can use to redirect if a question tries to pull you off track.
Step 9: Get a Visual Direction Brief
Ask Claude to suggest a visual direction for your slides — color palette, font pairings, layout principles, image style, and chart types. This won't design the slides for you, but it gives you (or your designer) a clear creative brief to work from.
Step 10: Stress-Test the Whole Thing
Finally, paste your full outline back into Claude and ask it to critique it as if it were a skeptical audience member. It should flag weak arguments, confusing slides, moments where attention might drop, and claims that need stronger evidence. Then ask it to rewrite the 3 weakest sections.

Tips
Work through the steps in order — each one builds on the previous. Be specific with your inputs: the more context you give Claude (your audience, your goal, your constraints), the better the output. And don't treat the results as final — use them as a strong draft that you refine with your own judgment.
