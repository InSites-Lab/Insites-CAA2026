### Table E. Enabler Analysis -- Key Questions

**1. Does notation work as an epistemic enabler?**

Of the 7 bot-marked claims, 1 was reframed by the expert (C013, 14.3% intervention rate for marked claims). Of the 35 unmarked claims, 2 were rejected + 2 expert-added + 1 reframed = 5 interventions (14.3% intervention rate for unmarked claims). The intervention rates are identical (14.3% for both marked and unmarked), suggesting that notation did NOT predict expert reaction in this session. However, the one case where notation intersected with expert intervention (C013) is notable: the bot marked it as inferred but the expert caught that the framing went beyond what inferred should cover -- the expert essentially performed a notation upgrade (inferred to thought-equivalent) by demanding source-faithful language. This suggests notation was functional in one case but at the wrong level.

**2. Does governance catch what notation misses?**

Of the 12 under-marked claims, 0 were rejected or challenged by the expert. The expert caught 0/12 under-marked claims through direct intervention. However, the expert's broader directive to "use a more suggestive tone when evidence is not clear" (applied at Stage 1) implicitly addressed the under-marking pattern without targeting specific claims. Governance operated at the meta-level (tone directive) rather than the claim level. This means 12 under-marked claims passed through both notation AND governance uncorrected -- a gap in the system.

**3. What is the error asymmetry?**

Under-marking: 12 claims (9 should-be-inferred + 3 should-be-thought). Over-marking: 0 claims. The asymmetry is entirely one-directional: the bot is systematically overconfident, presenting uncertain claims as established. This matches the bot's own self-diagnosis in the HITL analysis: "I default toward producing resolved, structured analysis. Unresolved ambiguity feels like an incomplete output."

**4. Which content types are most dangerous?**

- Overreach: 1/1 rejected (100.0% error rate) -- highest risk
- Inference: 1/19 rejected (5.3%) -- the rejected C010 was an inference
- Synthesis: 0/20 rejected (0.0%) -- no synthesis claims rejected
- Extraction: 0/2 rejected (0.0%)

Overreach is the only content type that produced rejection, but with n=1 the finding is indicative, not statistical. The more actionable finding is that inference claims (19/42 = 45.2% of all claims) are under-marked at a higher rate than synthesis claims: 7/15 unmarked inferences should have been marked (46.7%), vs. 5/17 unmarked syntheses (29.4%).

**5. Does the bot improve across stages?**

Stage 1-2 accuracy: 15/21 = 71.4%. Stage 3-4 accuracy: 7/14 = 50.0%. Stage 5-6 accuracy: 4/5 = 80.0%. Accuracy drops in the middle stages (3-4), which is the opposite of improvement. Stages 3 and 4 involve the most interpretive analytical work (Nara Grid assessments, comparative judgments), which appears to be where the bot's marking discipline breaks down. The Stage 5-6 recovery may reflect the expert's cumulative corrections having a calibrating effect, or may simply reflect the small sample (5 claims).

### Table F. Key Findings

1. **The bot produces a high volume of synthesis and inference, but marks only a fraction.** 39 of 42 claims (92.9%) are synthesis or inference; only 7 of those 39 (17.9%) carry any epistemic marker. The bot's notation rate of 16.7% significantly understates the degree of interpretive work in the output. (7/42, 16.7% marking rate vs. 92.9% non-extraction content)

2. **The expert's acceptance rate is high (85.7%), but expert interventions are concentrated at points of conceptual rather than factual error.** Both rejections (C010, C032) involved the bot applying an analytical framework incorrectly -- not getting facts wrong. The political context was irrelevant (misapplied scope); the "authenticity dilemma" was a misapplied concept (conservation threat, not authenticity question). This suggests the bot's primary failure mode is analytical framing, not factual accuracy. (2/2 rejections = conceptual error, 0/2 = factual error)

3. **Under-marking is systematic and one-directional.** 12 of 35 unmarked claims (34.3%) should have carried inferred or thought markers. Zero claims were over-marked. The bot is consistently overconfident, presenting multi-source syntheses and interpretive inferences as if they were explicit in sources. Neither the notation system nor expert governance caught this pattern at the individual claim level -- the expert addressed it only through a general tone directive. (12/35 under-marked, 0/7 over-marked)

4. **Notation accuracy degrades in the most interpretive stages.** Stages 3-4 (Nara Grid + Comparison), which require the most analytical judgment, show 50.0% accuracy -- compared to 71.4% in Stages 1-2 and 80.0% in Stages 5-6. The bot appears to lose marking discipline precisely when the analytical demands are highest. (7/14 correct in Stages 3-4 vs. 15/21 in Stages 1-2)

5. **Stage 5 deduplication is aggressive but justified.** Only 2 of the significance statement's analytical moves were genuinely new -- both expert-directed (collective cohesion framing, monumental presence). The remaining Stage 5 content restates claims from Stages 1-4. This confirms the codebook's guidance that Stage 5 should be the primary deduplication target, and suggests that the bot's contribution in the synthesis stage is structural (weaving) rather than analytical (new claims). (2/2 Stage 5 claims = expert-added)
