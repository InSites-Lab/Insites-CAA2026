🔍 1.1 Site Description

The Tuba-Zangariyye dolmen field occupies a rocky basalt hill (260–280 m above sea level) on the Korazim Plateau, in the Jordan Rift Valley between the Sea of Galilee and the Hula Valley in northern Israel. The hill forms part of an elongated spur extending between the outlets of Nahal Tovim and Wadi Abu Lauza, southeast of the village of Tuba Zangariyye. Four springs — ʿEn Zifan on the hill's northwestern flank, ʿEn ʿAdaya approximately 1 km west, and ʿEn Tovim and ʿEn Ha-Nasi about 0.7 km east — surround the spur. [A:p.1; B]

The hill carries at least 43 documented dolmens, clustering in two main concentrations: one along the northeastern slope (Dolmens 1–14) and a denser group on the hilltop itself (Dolmens 20–41), which accounts for roughly half the total. Smaller clusters lie slightly west of the summit (Dolmens 15–19) and on the southern slope (Dolmens 42–43). Together with associated tumuli, cairns, and stone circles, the broader field encompasses over 65 dolmens and more than 20 tumuli across approximately 0.5 km². [A:p.2; B; C:p.43]

The dolmens were constructed using locally available basalt — the geological signature of the Korazim Plateau, formed by Pliocene–Pleistocene lava flows. Most follow Zohar's Type B plan: a rectangular burial chamber roofed with stone slabs and enclosed by a ring wall or cairn. Covering stones average 0.5 × 1.5 × 3 m, though individual slabs may weigh up to approximately 15 tons. Two dolmens (Nos. 35 and 41) belong to the "Tortoise Shell" type — closed oval structures with corbelled roofing — which is a local variant unique to the Korazim Plateau. [A:p.3; B; C:pp.42–43]

Cupmarks hewn in bedrock appear next to four dolmens (Nos. 1, 8, 18, 22), likely contemporary with dolmen construction as part of funerary ritual. Mancala-like gameboards were carved into the roofing slabs of four others (Nos. 19, 28, 37, 43); these are probably later additions by shepherds or travelers who reused the dolmens as resting or meeting places. [A:p.3]

The dolmens date to the Intermediate Bronze Age and Middle Bronze Age II (ca. 2200–1900 BCE), based on typological parallels and excavation finds from other Korazim Plateau sites — including IB–MBIIA pottery and a diagnostic bronze chisel axe found in a dolmen north of ancient Korazim. No excavation has been conducted within the Tuba-Zangariyye field itself. [A:p.3–4; C:pp.46–47]

A later phase of agricultural activity — clearance heaps, stone field walls, terraces, animal pens, and watchtowers — overlays the megalithic landscape. Cartographic evidence from the Mandatory-era Survey of Palestine (1942) and comparisons with nearby excavated sites (Tel Yaʿaf South, Horbat Zalaf, Korazim) suggest this phase dates primarily to the Ottoman period and the British Mandate era, though it may have begun earlier. A road segment (Road 316) traversing the southern part of the hill appears connected to the Rosh Pinna–eastward route marked on the 1942 map. [A:pp.3–4]

The hilltop has suffered damage from the construction of a modern sheep pen, which likely destroyed additional dolmens, and from illegal burial of refuse on the northern part. [A:p.1] Despite this, the field remains one of the densest and best-preserved dolmen concentrations on the Korazim Plateau. [A:p.3; B]

🌐 Contexts

Geographic — The site sits on a basalt spur at the junction of two wadi outlets, flanked by four springs within a 1 km radius, in the Jordan Rift Valley between the Sea of Galilee and the Hula Valley. This position — on a water-rich plateau straddling two major geographical basins — frames the field as occupying a resource node within a landscape corridor that has sustained both sedentary and mobile populations since at least the Chalcolithic period. The geographic setting of the field, in turn, reframes the Korazim Plateau itself as a zone where megalithic mortuary investment concentrates specifically at well-watered spur locations. [A:p.1; C:p.40] 🔗 Planning: Spring environments and wadi outlets must be considered in any development buffer around the field — hydrological changes could affect both the setting and long-term stability of basalt structures.

Landscape — The hill commands wide vistas: the Golan Heights dolmen fields are visible from the site, and the Tuba-Zangariyye concentration itself is visible from surrounding terrain due to prominent capstones. [A:p.2–3; C:p.42] This inter-visibility between dolmen clusters across the Korazim Plateau and the Golan suggests a deliberately constructed visual network linking burial sites across the rift. The recognition of Tuba-Zangariyye as a particularly dense node in this network reframes the surrounding dolmen fields — at Shamir in the northern Hula Valley, and on the Golan — as participants in a shared mortuary landscape rather than isolated clusters. [A:p.3; B] 🔗 Planning: Viewshed protection is critical — not only the dolmen structures but the visual relationships between this field and other dolmen concentrations constitute heritage value. Development should be assessed for visual intrusion on these sightlines.

Historical — Two broad phases bracket the site's chronological depth: a Bronze Age megalithic phase (IB–MBII, ca. 2200–1900 BCE) and an Ottoman–Mandate agricultural phase. Between them lies a poorly documented gap of roughly three millennia during which the dolmens persisted as physical landmarks that attracted periodic engagement — Roman-Byzantine pottery at plateau dolmens, possible biblical and New Testament references to "tombs" and "giants" in the Korazim area. [A:pp.3–4; B; C:pp.45, 50 note 2] The historical context frames the dolmens as structures whose significance has been continuously reinterpreted across cultures — from original burial, through biblical narrative, to modern archaeological research — and the site's long interpretive afterlife, in turn, positions the Korazim dolmen phenomenon as a case study in the deep-time persistence of mortuary monuments.

Social — Stepansky links the dolmen builders to a semi-nomadic pastoralist population sustaining in the area during the IB–MBIIA period, based on the discovery of the nearby Horbat Berekh settlement with its animal pens, flint sickles, and grinding stones. [C:pp.46–48] The Korazim Plateau continued to host semi-nomadic cattle herders through historical periods, and until recently the Zangariyye and El-Heib Bedouin tribes grazed the area — including the dolmen field itself. [B; C:p.50 note 4] This social continuity — pastoralist populations using the same landscape over four millennia — frames the dolmen field not merely as Bronze Age mortuary architecture but as an enduring node in a pastoral territory. The Tuba-Zangariyye community's long-term presence on the plateau, in turn, carries heritage significance as the living context of the ancient landscape. 🔗 Planning: Engagement with the Tuba-Zangariyye community is essential — both for recording intangible heritage associated with the landscape and for integrating community perspectives into preservation planning.

Political — The management history of the site exposes a tension between development mandates and heritage protection within the Israeli legal framework. The Ministry of Housing proposed construction without consulting the IAA; the IAA initially approved salvage excavation (effectively consenting to destruction); only with the 2019 expansion did the IAA adopt a significance-based approach emphasizing in-situ preservation. [B] This trajectory frames the dolmen field as a test case for the shift from rescue-archaeology doctrine to values-based management in Israel. The case of Tuba-Zangariyye, in turn, reframes the broader debate about Israeli antiquities legislation — demonstrating that the existing law lacks adequate tools for designating sites for preservation when they fall within development zones. [B]

Technological — The construction of basalt dolmens weighing up to ~15 tons, using locally sourced orthostatic slabs, required coordinated labor, knowledge of stone transport mechanics, and an understanding of structural stacking and roofing — including the corbelling technique in the Tortoise Shell variants. [A:p.3; B; C:pp.42–43] Source C notes that the builders exploited naturally upright slabs in the field to facilitate construction, suggesting pragmatic material intelligence. [C:p.43] This technological context frames each dolmen as evidence of organizational capacity within what is otherwise characterized as a "modest" semi-nomadic society — and the investment required, in turn, raises the question of what motivated such disproportionate effort in mortuary architecture. [C:p.48]

Archaeological — The site has been investigated exclusively through surface survey — no dolmen at Tuba-Zangariyye has been excavated. All chronological and cultural attribution depends on regional parallels: Turville-Petre's 1931 excavations near Korazim, Epstein's Golan work, and Stepansky's finds from a dolmen north of Korazim. [A; C] The survey methodology (GPS at 0.2 m precision, ArcMap processing) is documented, but the data appendix was not uploaded. [A:p.2] This archaeological context frames what is known as heavily mediated by analogy — the specific field remains an unexcavated sample within a regionally dated phenomenon. The Tuba-Zangariyye field, in turn, reframes the research agenda for the Korazim Plateau by offering a dense, relatively well-preserved concentration that could yield the sealed contexts needed for independent absolute dating.

Thematic: Megalithic mortuary landscapes of the southern Levant — The Korazim Plateau field is described as the western extension of the vast megalithic field of southern Syria and Upper Jordan, and shares typological, chronological, and cultural traits with the Golan Heights dolmens. [C:pp.40, 44] Within this macro-region, the Tuba-Zangariyye concentration is characterized as outstanding in both number and density, and is proposed alongside the Shamir field in the northern Hula Valley as a possible regional cultural center. [A:p.3] This thematic context frames the Tuba field as a potential focal node within a trans-Jordan megalithic tradition. The identification of such a center at Tuba, in turn, reframes the Shamir and Golan fields as parts of a hierarchically organized network rather than an undifferentiated scatter. 🔗 Planning: Any regional heritage strategy for Levantine dolmens should treat the Tuba-Zangariyye field as a priority site, given its proposed role as a regional center and its position bridging the Korazim and Golan dolmen zones.

Intangible Heritage° — Biblical narratives about giants (Rephaim) in eastern Transjordan and the New Testament "tombs" near Korazim may reference dolmens of this area. The local Bedouin name for dolmens — "Dan" (shelter) — possibly preserves the New Testament tradition of outcasts living among the tombs. Jewish Talmudic literature refers to dolmens as "Merkolis" (related to Mercury), treating them as pagan entities. [C:p.50 note 2; B] These layers of intangible association — pre-Israelite myth, Christian narrative, rabbinic prohibition, and Bedouin oral tradition — frame the dolmens as generators of cultural meaning far beyond their original funerary function. The survival of these narratives, in turn, reinforces the dolmen landscape's role as a persistent locus of cultural imagination.

⚠ Critical Gap

The Zangariyye tribe has inhabited the plateau since at least the 18th century, and the dolmen field sits immediately adjacent to their village — yet no source records any community perspective on the site's significance, attitudes toward the development plan, or local knowledge of the landscape. Source B explicitly acknowledges that "community participation" remains "another challenge for the future" [B]. This gap affects the social context directly and may limit the legitimacy of any significance statement that claims landscape or social values.

💡 Brief: Stage 1 identified seven active contexts — geographic (spring-fed basalt spur), landscape (inter-visible dolmen network), historical (4,000-year two-phase trajectory), social (semi-nomadic pastoralist builders, Bedouin continuity), technological (megalithic construction by a mobile society), archaeological (unexcavated archive-in-potential), thematic (trans-Jordan megalithic tradition), and intangible (biblical, Talmudic, Bedouin narrative layers). 2.0 Values: Identification and Analysis

1. Scientific Value — "An Unexcavated Archive at the Densest Node"

The Tuba-Zangariyye field's most consequential value may be what it has not yet yielded. No dolmen at this site has been excavated, meaning that sealed burial chambers, stratigraphic fills, and associated deposits remain intact. [A; C] The field's density (43+ dolmens in ~0.5 km²) and typological diversity (Type B and the locally unique Tortoise Shell variant) offer a concentrated sample that could, if investigated with current or future methods — aDNA, micro-morphology, OSL — produce the first independent chronological and biological data for this specific concentration. [°: inferred from unexcavated status + regional research trajectory, A:p.3; C:pp.46–48] The archaeological context (Stage 1) frames this as preservation-as-archive: the intact deposits are themselves a primary record whose research value would diminish upon excavation, even if well-documented. The field's proposed status as a possible regional center [A:p.3] amplifies this — sealed contexts here could clarify whether density correlates with social differentiation among dolmen-building communities.

2. Historical Value — "Persistent Monuments in a Changing Landscape"

The dolmens represent tangible evidence of mortuary practice during the IB–MBII transition (ca. 2200–1900 BCE), a period characterized by the dominance of semi-nomadic pastoralist groups in the southern Levant. [C:pp.44–45] The two-phase timeline (Stage 1) — megalithic burial followed by Ottoman–Mandate agricultural overlay — brackets a roughly 3,000-year gap during which the structures persisted as landmarks, attracting later engagement (Roman-Byzantine sherds at plateau dolmens, possible mancala gameboards carved by shepherds). [A:p.3; C:p.45] This persistence — monumental structures outlasting the society that built them and accumulating new associations across cultures — constitutes historical value not only for the Bronze Age phase but for the entire trajectory of reinterpretation.

3. Landscape Value — "A Visually Connected Mortuary Territory"

The hilltop position, prominent capstones, and inter-visibility with other dolmen concentrations on the Korazim Plateau and the Golan Heights suggest that the field's placement was deliberate — oriented toward visual connection with the broader megalithic landscape. [A:pp.2–3; C:p.42] The landscape context (Stage 1) frames this as a possible network of mortuary sites linked by sightlines, though whether this visual relationship was intentionally designed or is an incidental product of hilltop placement remains uncertain 💭. Source A proposes that the "central location… and the advantageous vistas" indicate importance [A:p.3], but alternative explanations — favorable geology, gentle slope for stone transport — cannot be excluded. Nevertheless, the surviving visual relationships between the field and surrounding dolmen clusters constitute landscape value regardless of original intent.

4. Technological Value — "Megalithic Construction by a Mobile Society"

Each dolmen represents a significant engineering effort: quarrying, transporting, and stacking basalt slabs weighing up to an estimated 15 tons, using dry-stone technique without mortar. [B; C:p.42] The Tortoise Shell type (Dolmens 35, 41) adds corbelling — a roofing method that requires progressive inward stacking of 5–7 slabs and appears, on this plateau, to be a local innovation. [A:p.3; C:pp.43–44] Source C notes that builders exploited naturally upright orthostatic slabs, suggesting pragmatic material selection. [C:p.43] The technological context (Stage 1) frames the disproportionate investment — monumental construction by communities described as leading a "modest" semi-nomadic life [C:p.48] — as evidence of social organization and technical knowledge that the surviving material culture (stone tools, coarse pottery, grinding stones from Horbat Berekh) would not otherwise suggest.

5. Social Value° — "Pastoralist Continuity and Community Presence"

Stepansky links the dolmen builders to semi-nomadic pastoralists of the IB–MBIIA period, based on Horbat Berekh's material culture. [C:pp.46–48] The Korazim Plateau has sustained pastoral communities through historical periods, and the Zangariyye and El-Heib Bedouin tribes have inhabited it since at least the 18th century. [C:p.50 note 4; B] The dolmen field sits immediately adjacent to the present village. This long arc of pastoral presence — ancient builders, Ottoman-era cultivators, modern Bedouin — suggests a social value rooted in continuity of landscape use, though the connection between the Bronze Age population and later inhabitants is cultural-geographic rather than demonstrated lineage. [°] The critical gap noted in Stage 1 applies here: no community voice has been recorded, and the social value therefore rests on archaeological inference rather than living testimony.

6. Intangible Heritage Value° — "Layers of Narrative Across Traditions"

Biblical references to Rephaim giants in Transjordan, the New Testament "tombs" near Korazim, Talmudic references to dolmens as "Merkolis" (pagan entities), and the Bedouin term "Dan" (shelter) for dolmens collectively suggest that these structures have generated cultural meaning across at least four distinct traditions. [C:p.50 note 2; B] The intangible context (Stage 1) frames the dolmens as persistent stimuli for narrative production. However, the evidence linking these specific textual traditions to the Tuba-Zangariyye field (rather than to Korazim Plateau dolmens generally) is indirect 💭 — the association is plausible given proximity but not site-specific.

The primary relationship between values is reinforcing cohesion rather than tension. Landscape value (visual connectivity) and technological value (megalithic construction) converge in the same physical features — the prominent capstones that create visual presence are also the engineering achievement.

One potential tension exists between scientific value and development pressure: the 2015 plan would have destroyed most dolmens through salvage excavation — a process that, even when professionally conducted, diminishes the preservation-as-archive value.
